## Population Outlook

Muscat Governorate's population stood at **1.50 million** at end-2024 (NCSI). Using three growth scenarios calibrated against recent NCSI trends and Oman Vision 2040 drivers, we project:

| Scenario | Annual Rate | 2030 | 2040 | Growth |
|----------|------------|------|------|--------|
| **Low Growth** | 1.5% | 1.64M | **1.90M** | +27% |
| **Base Case** | 2.5% | 1.74M | **2.23M** | +48% |
| **High Growth** | 4.0% | 1.90M | **2.81M** | +87% |

**Key driver:** Expat labor demand — expatriates constitute 61% of Muscat's population. Economic diversification under Vision 2040 is the primary swing factor between scenarios.

The population range matters because each sector modeled here scales non-linearly in planning terms. A city that adds 700,000 residents under the base case does not just need incremental services. It needs land, staffing pipelines, financing, and delivery capacity that must be sequenced years in advance.

## Infrastructure Risk: Healthcare

Muscat currently has an estimated **2,500 hospital beds** (1.67 per 1,000 people) — well below the WHO benchmark of 3.0 and the GCC average of 2.0.

| Scenario | Beds Needed (WHO 3.0) | Shortfall | Capacity Exceeded |
|----------|-----------------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Low Growth | 5,708 | **2,808 beds** | Already |
| Base Case | 6,678 | **3,778 beds** | Already |
| High Growth | 8,425 | **5,525 beds** | Already |

**Critical finding:** Even with 400 planned beds (by 2028), Muscat's hospital bed ratio **already falls short of both WHO and GCC benchmarks today**. By 2040, the base case requires nearly **tripling** current capacity.

This is not a long-dated problem. The modeled breakpoint is immediate because current capacity is already below planning benchmarks. The main policy implication is that healthcare expansion needs to start from backlog reduction, not from a clean 2024 baseline.

## Infrastructure Risk: Education

Muscat has approximately **330 schools** serving ~300,000 school-age students (20% of population). Current density is 900 students/school — well above the 600 student quality benchmark.

| Scenario | Students 2040 | New Schools Needed | New Teachers (15:1) |
|----------|--------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Low Growth | 380K | +92 to +304 | +10,376 |
| Base Case | 445K | +164 to +412 | +14,685 |
| High Growth | 562K | +294 to +606 | +22,452 |

**Critical finding:** Under the base case, Muscat needs **~400 new schools** to reach quality benchmarks and **~15,000 additional teachers** by 2040.

Education pressure grows steadily rather than suddenly, but the implementation burden is large. New school delivery requires land release, construction phasing, operating budgets, and a sustained teacher recruitment pipeline. Delaying action compresses those requirements into a shorter and more expensive window.

## Infrastructure Risk: Water and Utilities

The water model estimates Muscat's effective desalinated supply at **280 MLD in 2024**, increasing to **580 MLD** after planned additions, including the Al Ghubrah 3 expansion. Demand is modeled from population, a **180 liters/person/day** planning assumption, and a **1.15 safety factor**.

| Scenario | Water Demand 2040 | Capacity 2040 | Gap / Reserve |
|----------|------------------:|--------------:|--------------:|
| Low Growth | 393.0 MLD | 580 MLD | **+187.0 MLD reserve** |
| Base Case | 462.4 MLD | 580 MLD | **+117.6 MLD reserve** |
| High Growth | 581.7 MLD | 580 MLD | **-1.7 MLD deficit** |

**Critical finding:** Water is the strongest-performing sector under the base case because planned capacity materially changes the outlook. However, that result depends on delivery of the announced supply expansion and leaves far less buffer under high growth.

The sector should therefore be read as a resilience warning, not as evidence that no action is required. In high-growth conditions, Muscat effectively uses up the post-expansion margin by 2040. Any delay in project delivery, higher per-capita demand, or drought-related operating stress would tighten the position further.

---

## Breakpoint Years

Both social sectors are **already under capacity strain** in 2024:

- **Healthcare:** Current beds/1,000 ratio (1.67) is below both WHO (3.0) and GCC (2.0) benchmarks
- **Education:** Current student density (900/school) exceeds the 600-student quality target

For water, the modeled base case does **not** breach capacity after the planned expansion. The high-growth case reaches effective balance by 2040, meaning the utility system has much less room for delay or planning error than the headline reserve may suggest.

The gap **widens every year** without investment. Delay compounds the deficit.

## Cross-Sector Analysis

The common theme across all three sectors is that moderate population growth translates into significant infrastructure consequences. Healthcare and education show immediate and persistent deficits because current service levels are already tight relative to benchmark capacity. Water appears more manageable only because a major expansion is assumed to arrive early in the forecast period. Taken together, the model shows that Muscat does not need extreme growth to face planning pressure. The base case alone is sufficient to create large delivery requirements across land use, capital budgets, staffing, and procurement.

Another cross-sector pattern is sequencing risk. Physical infrastructure can sometimes be built faster than institutions can recruit staff, establish operating models, and absorb new assets into the service network. That means school counts, bed counts, and desalination MLD should be treated as minimum capacity metrics, not as full readiness measures.

## Risk Matrix: If Growth Exceeds the Base Case

| Risk Area | Base Case Outlook | If Growth Exceeds Base | Planning Consequence |
|----------|-------------------|------------------------|----------------------|
| Population | 2.23M by 2040 | Moves toward 2.8M range | Planning baselines become obsolete earlier |
| Healthcare | Large deficit but still modelable within staged expansion logic | Bed shortfall widens beyond 5,500 under WHO benchmark | More aggressive PPPs, workforce imports, and phased hospital delivery needed |
| Education | Significant school and teacher build-out required | New-school requirement moves above 600 under quality target | Land allocation and teacher recruitment become the primary bottlenecks |
| Water | Reserve remains after planned expansion | Reserve is exhausted or turns negative | Additional desalination or demand management required before 2040 |
| Public finance | Capital spending already substantial | Multi-sector spending bunches in the same years | Higher risk of delivery slippage and trade-offs across sectors |

The high-growth scenario is therefore useful as a stress test. It shows which systems fail gracefully and which fail quickly. Healthcare and education deteriorate the fastest because their deficits are structural, while water is more sensitive to whether the planned addition arrives on time.

## Prioritized Recommendations

### 1. Expand healthcare capacity first

Healthcare has the clearest immediate deficit. Muscat should accelerate hospital capacity through a mix of public delivery and PPP mechanisms, with phased targets tied to population thresholds rather than one-off project announcements.

### 2. Lock in a long-range education build program

Education demand grows steadily and predictably. The right response is a rolling school pipeline, especially in high-growth wilayats, paired with teacher recruitment and training targets that begin well before facilities open.

### 3. Protect water resilience margins

Water performs relatively well in the base case only because planned capacity is assumed to arrive. Policymakers should monitor delivery milestones, preserve redundancy, and prepare contingent demand-management measures in case growth or consumption runs above assumptions.

### 4. Use one planning dashboard across sectors

Population-led planning should not sit in sector silos. A shared dashboard linked to updated NCSI figures would allow trigger-based investment decisions, helping ministries move from reactive expansion to staged, evidence-based planning.

### 5. Prioritize workforce and operating readiness

Infrastructure delivery is not just a construction issue. Hospital beds need clinicians, schools need teachers, and desalination plants need operating and maintenance capability. Workforce planning should therefore be treated as a first-order infrastructure dependency.

---

## Three Recommended Actions

1. **Healthcare — Accelerate hospital construction.** The current pipeline of 400 beds is insufficient. Muscat needs an additional **2,500–5,000 beds** by 2040 depending on growth. Prioritize PPP models to attract private hospital investment.

2. **Education — Launch a school-building program tied to population data.** Plan for **10–25 new schools per year** through 2040, with priority in high-growth wilayats (Bawshar, Seeb). Simultaneously recruit and train 1,000+ teachers annually.

3. **Establish a dynamic infrastructure planning unit.** Link NCSI population data directly to infrastructure capacity dashboards. Use scenario-based planning (as modeled here) to trigger investment thresholds automatically rather than reacting to shortages after they occur.

These original actions remain valid, but the water sector evidence adds an important refinement: some infrastructure systems can be stabilized with timely expansion, while others require structural catch-up. That distinction should shape capital allocation and policy urgency.

## Data Quality and Limitations

The model is designed for strategic planning rather than engineering design. Several Muscat-level infrastructure values are estimated from national totals, major facility references, and publicly reported project announcements where no single official governorate table is available.

Key limitations include:

- Population is modeled with compound annual growth rather than cohort-based demographic transitions
- Hospital bed counts and school counts are partially estimated from available public sources
- Water supply is simplified to effective capacity and does not model transmission losses, seasonal peaks, or industrial demand
- Planned additions are treated as delivered on time and at stated scale
- Benchmark comparisons are useful for planning, but they do not fully capture local service models or utilization differences

Even with these limitations, the directional conclusion is robust: moderate growth creates meaningful infrastructure pressure, and delayed action makes the response more expensive.

---

## Closing Note

Muscat's 2040 challenge is not whether growth happens. It is whether infrastructure planning can move early enough to absorb growth without sustained declines in service quality. The model indicates that healthcare and education require immediate strategic attention, while water requires disciplined execution to preserve resilience.
